MEMORANDUM

TO: Dr. Trevor Duston
FROM: Tony Capps
DATE: March 9, 2026
SUBJECT: The Lilac Dress (Fast Fashion Lifecycle)

Rhetorical Situation

Purpose

To show the truth behind fast fashion shopping. Instead of looking at that $12 dress as just a cute steal, I want everyone to understand it for what it really is, a symbol of environmental damage. By telling the story backward, I'm hoping to pull the curtain back on all the hidden costs (environmental and human) that usually get ignored when you're just clicking "add to cart."

Audience

I'm talking to Gen Z and young Millennials, especially the ones who love sharing "haul" videos and snagging the latest trends for cheap. These shoppers often claim to care about the environment, but there's a big difference between what they say and how they shop.

Message

My message takes shape through a Reverse Lifecycle approach. The story "The Lilac Dress" starts with the dress tossed away in a Chilean landfill, rewinds through its big party moment, its arrival in plastic packaging, and lands all the way back at its ugly beginnings in toxic dye vats and pesticide soaked cotton fields.

Thesis

I want to point out the environmental cost of fast fashion for trend driven young people by walking them backward through the toxic life story of a $12 dress.

Ethos (Credibility)

Visual

A comic stripe designed like a social media post with comments. The audience will trust a message if they feel like they can relate to it.

Real Facts

The giant piles of clothing waste in deserts and the way chemical dyes turn rivers purple.

Structure

The narrative was built using Dan Harmon's Story Circle. It's a pro move, making sure the story flows with purpose.

Pathos (Emotional Appeal)

By starting with the high of a party and moving backward to a grim factory, I want people to feel duped like brands sold them fake happiness.

Human Connection

The Heavy Price we focus on a factory worker's face. There's a shot that moves from the laborer's hand to the influencer's hand, making the pain real and personal.

Aesthetic Decay

The colors tell their own story. It starts with bright lilac, shifts to toxic neon, and ends in dull earth tones. As the colors fade, so does the glamour, it's unsettling, and that's the point.

Logos (Logical Progression)

The Three Acts

We begin at the landfill and the dress's rejection.
The party, the selfie, and the package reveal.
The factory, the shipping container, and the cotton field.

The dress starts out "comfortable" in the landfill, dives back into its troubled past, and finally returns to its origin as raw cotton. The story proves one thing: you only get that $12 price tag, at the price of something else.
